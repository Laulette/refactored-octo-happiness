For many people, the word "fencing" brings to mind one image, and one image only: people in white full-body suits wielding extremely thin and flexible swords in a highly-stylized and very strict sport. This is Olympic (Fédération Internationale d'Escrime, or FIE) fencing, and it is very different from historical rapier fencing.

There's nothing wrong with enjoying FIE fencing, but it's not exactly swordplay. The scoring systems used in FIE fencing have fairly little to do with the casualties that would be sustained in a real duel. In rapier fencing, points are won based on what part of the body is hit and, in some cases, whether the hit is a stab or a cut. If the injury would be fatal in a real duel, more points are awarded, whereas less significant hits would either be worth fewer points or would only put the affected body part out of use (one might lose the use of an arm or leg). Rapier fencing also allows either party to score at any point in the match—there are no rules requiring a rapier fencer to be advancing or be in a certain position before an attack.

The weapons are also vastly different, which affects technique. While plenty of elements of rapier fencing technique are visible in FIE fencing as well, they cannot be perfectly transferred over with the same results.
